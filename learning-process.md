
Question 1 — What is the Feynman Technique?
The Feynman Technique is a learning method in which we truly understand a concept by explaining it in simple, everyday words, as if we were teaching it to someone with no background in the subject.

Question 2 — In this video, what was the most interesting story or idea for you?
The idea that stood out the most to me was Barbara Oakley's explanation of how our brain operates in two different modes — focused mode and diffused mode — and how both are essential for effective learning. What I found especially fascinating was the example showing that difficult problems often become easier after taking a short break or even sleeping on them, because the brain quietly continues processing the information in the background. It made me realize that stepping away from a problem isn't wasted time; it's actually part of solving it.

Question 3 — What are the focused and diffused modes of thinking?
a. Focused mode is when we concentrate deeply on a specific task — solving a problem, reading carefully, or learning something step by step with full attention.
b. Diffused mode is a more relaxed state of thinking where the brain quietly makes connections between ideas in the background. It usually happens when we're resting, walking, taking a shower, or doing something light and unrelated to the task.
Both modes work together — focused mode builds the pieces, and diffused mode helps connect them.

Question 5 — What actions can you take going forward to improve your learning process?

Going forward, there are a few changes I'd genuinely like to make in the way I learn:

Deeper focus, fewer distractions. I'll try to cut down on social media and unnecessary phone notifications during study time, so my focused mode actually gets the attention it needs.

Learn by explaining. I want to practice explaining concepts in my own words instead of just memorizing them. If I can't explain it simply, I probably haven't understood it.

More hands-on practice. Especially with coding, I'll spend more time actually building things rather than just reading about them, and try to finish assignments on time instead of pushing them off.

Communicate early when stuck. Instead of struggling silently for hours, I'll ask questions sooner — it saves time and usually leads to better understanding.

Stay consistent and curious. I want to treat learning as something enjoyable rather than stressful, and stay consistent even on days when motivation is low.

Build longer attention spans. Programming demands sustained focus, so I'd like to gradually train myself to concentrate for longer stretches without breaking flow.

Revise and teach. I plan to revisit concepts regularly and teach them to others whenever I get the chance, because teaching is one of the best ways to find the gaps in your own understanding.
