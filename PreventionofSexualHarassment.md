
1. What kinds of behaviour cause sexual harassment?

Sexual harassment means any unwanted behaviour of a sexual nature that makes a person feel uncomfortable, unsafe, or disrespected at work.

Verbal Harassment
• Making sexual jokes or comments.
• Commenting on someone's body, looks, or clothes.
• Repeatedly asking someone out after they have refused.
• Using insulting or sexual words.
• Spreading sexual rumours.

Visual Harassment
• Showing inappropriate pictures, videos, or messages.
• Sending sexual images, memes, or emails.
• Making obscene gestures.
• Staring at someone in an uncomfortable way.

Physical Harassment
• Unwanted touching, hugging, or patting.
• Blocking someone's path or cornering them.
• Any form of sexual assault.

Written Harassment
• Sending unwanted sexual messages, letters, emails, or notes.

Quid Pro Quo Harassment
• When a manager or person in authority asks for sexual favours in exchange for promotions, benefits, or job security.

Online/Cyber Harassment
• Sending unwanted sexual messages or images online.
• Making inappropriate comments on social media or workplace chats.

Even one serious incident can be considered sexual harassment.



2. What would you do in case you face or witness any incident or repeated incidents of such behaviour?

If I face it:
• Clearly tell the person that their behaviour is unwelcome and should stop.
• Keep a record of incidents, including dates, times, and any evidence such as messages or emails.
• Report the matter to the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) or HR.
• Seek support from trusted colleagues, mentors, or family members.
• Be aware of my rights under the PoSH Act, 2013.

If I witness it:
• Speak up if it is safe to do so.
• Support the affected person and encourage them to report the incident.
• Inform the ICC or HR about what I witnessed.
• Never ignore, encourage, or treat such behaviour as a joke.

Creating a safe and respectful workplace is everyone's responsibility.
